---
title: >-
  How do other countries, especially in Europe, handle offshore wind, and how
  does the U.S. compare?
date: '2026-04-30'
relatedKnowledge: []
---
The entire history of offshore wind begins in a small Danish town called Vindeby in 1991 — with 11 turbines, each barely powerful enough to light a few hundred homes.
That modest experiment off the island of Lolland was the world's first offshore wind farm, built by a Danish utility company that would eventually become Ørsted.
The electricity industry at the time thought putting turbines in saltwater was absurd. Within six years, those same turbines were outperforming expectations — and the skeptics went quiet.
Denmark spent the following decades refining the technology, the finance models and the permitting systems, building a 30-year head start that no other country has been able to fully close.
By 2024, wind power supplied roughly 59% of Denmark's entire electricity demand — more than any other country on Earth.
Denmark did this through consistent government policy: guaranteed prices for wind energy, long-term contracts, and public auctions where developers compete to build wind farms at the lowest cost.
That auction system — called a Contract for Difference, or CfD — is the standard model across Europe today. The government sets a guaranteed price floor; if the market price falls below it, the developer gets topped up. If it rises above, the developer pays the difference back.
This mechanism removes most of the financial risk for investors, which is why European developers are willing to commit billions to projects that take a decade to build.
The UK has used this model aggressively. In early 2026 it awarded 9.7 gigawatts of new wind capacity in a single auction round — enough to power millions of homes — drawing in major investment from across the world.
The UK now has nearly 17 gigawatts of offshore wind installed, making it the second-largest offshore wind market on the planet, behind only China.
Germany sits third globally, with about 9 gigawatts installed, and has set a national target of reaching 30 gigawatts of offshore wind by 2030.
The Netherlands has built large wind clusters in the North Sea — groups of adjacent wind farms sharing infrastructure — and crossed 4.7 gigawatts of installed offshore capacity by end of 2024.
Four North Sea countries — Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark and Belgium — jointly signed a declaration committing to at least 65 gigawatts of combined offshore wind by 2030 and 150 gigawatts by 2050.
That kind of multi-country coordination is possible because the North Sea is shared water. Countries are not just building for themselves — they are planning a connected regional energy grid.
Europe as a whole had about 38.6 gigawatts of offshore wind installed by end of 2025, accounting for roughly 45% of all offshore wind capacity worldwide.
The dominant company in this global industry is Denmark's Ørsted, which operates 10.2 gigawatts of offshore wind and has another 8.1 gigawatts under construction across multiple countries.
German utility RWE is the second largest, aggressively expanding through UK auctions. Swedish state company Vattenfall and Spain's Iberdrola round out the top players — all European.
The turbines themselves keep getting larger. The average offshore turbine installed in Europe in 2025 had a capacity of 10.7 megawatts. A single turbine of this size can power roughly 10,000 homes on its own.
The world's largest offshore wind farm under active construction is Hornsea 3, off the Yorkshire coast of England — a 2.9-gigawatt project designed to supply over 3.3 million British homes.
Now compare all of this to the United States. The first offshore wind farm in America — five turbines off Block Island, Rhode Island — didn't open until 2016, twenty-five years after Denmark's first farm.
The US had no large-scale offshore wind until South Fork Wind opened in late 2023. As of early 2026, the total US offshore wind capacity sits at roughly 2 gigawatts — less than what the Netherlands alone has built.
The US has enormous potential. Its Atlantic coastline sits close to major population centers, its winds are strong and consistent, and 80% of US electricity demand comes from coastal states.
But where Europe built up three decades of policy frameworks, supply chains and specialized ports, the US spent that same time debating whether to start.
The Biden administration tried to accelerate. It set a target of 30 gigawatts of US offshore wind by 2030 and issued dozens of new federal leases. The industry began investing heavily in response.
Then the Trump administration returned in 2025 and halted all new offshore wind permitting on federal waters — the same waters covering most of the viable US coastline.
By early 2026 the US had spent nearly $2 billion in taxpayer money paying companies to abandon their wind leases and redirect investment into fossil fuels instead.
China, by contrast, has moved in the opposite direction. It now leads the world with over 41 gigawatts of offshore wind installed and is building new capacity at a pace no other country can match.
In 2025 alone, China added roughly 65% of all new offshore wind capacity installed globally that year. Its turbine manufacturers are now the world's largest and cheapest.
The companies and engineers that Europe and China have been training for decades — in turbine design, cable installation, port logistics — are the same ones now competing for contracts everywhere else in the world.
The US has the coastline, the wind and the electricity demand to be a global offshore wind leader. What it lacks, as of 2026, is the political will to build the industry that Denmark started with 11 small turbines in 1991.
