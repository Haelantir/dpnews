---
title: 'Why does Trump oppose wind power, and who benefits from that stance?'
date: '2026-04-30'
relatedKnowledge: []
---
Trump's hostility toward wind power didn't start as a political position. It started as a personal grudge.
In 2006, Trump bought a coastal estate in Aberdeenshire, Scotland, to build what he called the world's greatest golf course.
Shortly after, a local energy group announced plans to build an offshore wind farm about 3.5 kilometers from his resort — close enough to be clearly visible from the fairways.
Trump said at the time: "I want to see the ocean. I do not want to see windmills." He filed formal objections with the Scottish government.
He testified before the Scottish Parliament in 2012, calling the turbines ugly and environmentally irresponsible, and threatened to cancel the entire golf development.
Scotland approved the wind farm anyway. Trump sued all the way to the UK Supreme Court — and lost unanimously in 2015.
The turbines were built. They are still visible from his golf course today. That legal defeat appears to have never left him.
Since then, his rhetoric against wind has escalated far beyond property disputes. He has claimed turbines cause cancer, drive whales insane, and are destroying America's landscape.
None of these claims have scientific backing. Health agencies and marine researchers have consistently found no credible evidence supporting them.
When Trump returned to the White House in January 2025, stopping wind energy became an actual government policy — not just campaign rhetoric.
On his first day back in office, he signed an executive order declaring a national energy emergency. The order explicitly named oil, gas and coal as energy — and excluded wind and solar.
His administration halted all new offshore wind leasing and permitting on federal lands and waters, freezing dozens of planned projects.
Federal courts struck down several of these orders, ruling them arbitrary and unlawful. But the administration found another way.
The Trump administration began paying energy companies to voluntarily abandon their wind leases — and reinvest that money into fossil fuels instead.
The French company TotalEnergies was the first, walking away from its U.S. offshore wind projects in exchange for a reimbursement deal. It redirected its investment into liquefied natural gas.
By April 2026 the administration had spent nearly $2 billion in taxpayer money on these buyout deals — paying companies to not build clean energy.
Critics noted the irony: Trump spent years attacking wind energy subsidies as wasteful government spending, then used government money to dismantle the industry entirely.
So who benefits from all this? The answer starts with campaign money.
In April 2024, Trump invited oil and gas executives to his Mar-a-Lago club and reportedly told them: donate $1 billion to my campaign, and I'll give you everything on your policy wish list.
The fossil fuel industry responded. By election day, oil and gas interests had directed roughly $450 million toward Trump and Republican candidates through donations, lobbying and outside spending.
Once Trump won, the returns came quickly. His administration reversed a pause on liquefied natural gas export approvals, opened federal lands to new drilling, and installed fossil fuel executives into key government roles.
Chris Wright, the CEO of a fracking company called Liberty Energy, was appointed to run the Department of Energy — the agency that oversees the nation's entire energy strategy.
The top 15 fossil fuel billionaires saw their combined personal wealth rise by over $40 billion in the months following Trump's election.
When wind energy loses federal support, natural gas fills the gap. Gas plants can be built quickly, and utility companies locked out of wind contracts turn to gas suppliers instead.
Electricity demand in the U.S. is rising fast — driven partly by data centers powering AI — and every new wind project that gets blocked creates a market opening for fossil fuel producers.
The political logic runs in the other direction too. Wind and solar projects tend to create jobs in swing states and rural areas, building local support for clean energy that threatens the fossil fuel coalition long-term.
Republican voters have followed Trump's lead. In 2020, roughly half of Republicans supported expanding wind power. By 2025, that number had dropped to 48 percent — and support for offshore oil drilling among Republicans had risen to 75 percent.
Some analysts argue this is less about energy economics and more about identity — wind turbines have become a symbol of climate policy, elite coastal values and government overreach in conservative media.
Meanwhile the wind farms already built keep spinning. Federal courts have repeatedly blocked the administration's attempts to stop construction on projects already underway.
The turbines visible from Trump's Scottish golf course are still there too — generating clean power for British homes, whether he likes it or not.
