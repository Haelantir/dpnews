---
title: How does electricity from an offshore wind farm actually reach homes on land?
date: '2026-04-30'
relatedKnowledge: []
---
Wind blows over the ocean, pushing against the massive blades of an offshore turbine and making them spin. / Each blade can stretch over 100 meters — longer than a football field — to catch as much wind as possible. / The spinning blades rotate a shaft inside a box called the nacelle, which sits at the very top of the tower. / Inside the nacelle, a generator converts that spinning motion into electricity, the same basic principle as a bicycle dynamo. / The electricity produced at this point is alternating current (AC) — the same kind that flows through the outlets in your home. / From the base of each turbine, cables run down the tower and are buried under the seabed, connecting all turbines together like a chain. / These cables linking the turbines together are called array cables, and they carry electricity to a central hub offshore. / That central hub is called an offshore substation — a large steel platform fixed to the ocean floor, roughly the size of a small office building. / At the offshore substation, a transformer raises the voltage — think of voltage as the pressure pushing electricity through a wire. / Higher voltage means the electricity travels more efficiently over long distances, losing less energy as heat along the way. / For farms located far from shore, the electricity is converted from AC to DC (direct current) before the long underwater journey begins. / DC is preferred for long-distance underwater transmission because it loses far less energy than AC over extended cable runs. / The cables carrying this high-voltage electricity from the offshore substation to land are called export cables, and they are buried beneath the seabed. / The world's largest offshore wind farm, Hornsea 3, uses an export cable that travels from the North Sea to the UK coast and then runs more than 50 kilometers underground to a converter station inland. / When the export cable reaches the shoreline, engineers drill a horizontal tunnel under the beach rather than digging it up — a technique called horizontal directional drilling. / The drill starts from a small pit behind the dunes and bores a path beneath the beach until the drill head emerges from the seabed half a mile to a mile out at sea. / Where the undersea cable meets the inland cable, they are joined inside an underground concrete box called a transition joint bay, typically built beneath a parking lot and invisible once complete. / From that junction, cables continue underground along roads to an onshore substation, usually located a few miles inland. / At the onshore substation, DC electricity is converted back into AC — because the existing power grid that serves homes runs on AC. / The substation also adjusts the voltage back down to levels the local grid can safely handle and distribute. / From the onshore substation, electricity enters the regional power grid — the same network that carries power from coal plants, solar farms and gas turbines. / The grid doesn't label where electricity comes from. When you switch on a light, you're drawing from a shared pool of power that includes all these sources at once. / One full rotation of a large offshore turbine generates enough electricity to cover a typical home's power use for roughly 20 hours. / A single wind farm using 15 MW turbines with a total capacity of 2 gigawatts needs just over 130 turbines to power around 1.5 million homes per year. / Nearly 80 percent of U.S. electricity demand comes from coastal and Great Lakes states, meaning offshore wind can connect to the grid through relatively short transmission lines. / Offshore turbines produce more power than land-based ones because ocean winds blow stronger and more consistently, with no hills, trees or buildings blocking them. / Subsea cables face real physical threats — fishing trawlers, anchors and shifting bedrock all cause damage, and failures are expensive and complex to repair. / Each cable is heavily armored with layers of steel wires, waterproof insulation and protective sheathing, making them look more like a thick rope than a wire. / The entire system — from turbine blade to your wall outlet — can span hundreds of kilometers and involves dozens of specialized components all working in sequence. / None of it is visible from shore. The beach looks the same, and the only clue that something runs beneath it might be a few manhole covers in a parking lot nearby.
